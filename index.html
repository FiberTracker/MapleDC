Why Sovereign Compute Matters
Canadian Data Residency Imperative
Federal government and critical infrastructure data increasingly required to reside on Canadian-owned infrastructure, outside the reach of the U.S. CLOUD Act. This creates structural demand for Canadian-owned DC operators.5
Key Policy Developments
CLOUD Act Exposure — The Core Issue
Enacted: Mar 2018 (US)
The U.S. Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data Act allows U.S. law enforcement to compel U.S.-headquartered companies to produce data stored anywhere globally — including Canada. Any DC operator owned by a U.S. parent (Cologix/Stonepeak, Vantage/DigitalBridge, IREN, all hyperscalers) is subject to CLOUD Act data requests, regardless of where the server physically sits.6
Federal Sovereign Compute Program
Announced: Budget 2024 | Deadline: Feb 15, 2026
C$926M federal program to build sovereign AI compute capacity. Minimum threshold: 100MW per facility. Must be Canadian-owned and operated. Applications closed Feb 15, 2026. BCE applied via Queen's University (ON-1) and likely SK-1. Expected to fund 2-4 large facilities.7
Bill C-27 — Digital Charter Implementation Act
Status: Died on order paper (Parliament dissolved Jan 2025)
Would have created Canada's first comprehensive data privacy law (CPPA) with data residency implications. Died when Parliament dissolved. Expected to be reintroduced in modified form. Creates regulatory uncertainty that benefits sovereign-positioned operators who can market CLOUD Act immunity today.8
Quebec Data Sovereignty Requirements
Law 25 — In Effect
Quebec's Law 25 (modernized privacy law) imposes strict data localization requirements for Quebec public sector data. Creates captive demand for Quebec-based sovereign DCs. Benefits QScale, eStruxture, and BCE's eventual Quebec presence.9
Federal Pension DC Incentive (Proposed)
Status: Announced, not yet enacted
C$15B proposed incentive to attract Canadian pension fund investment into domestic DC infrastructure. Would create tax-advantaged vehicles similar to renewable energy yieldcos. CPP, CDPQ, OMERS, AIMCo all have growing digital infra allocations.10
EU Comparison — GDPR & Data Act
Benchmark Reference
EU's GDPR and Data Act provide the template Canada is moving toward. Key difference: EU has enacted comprehensive data sovereignty; Canada has patchwork (Quebec Law 25, federal PIPEDA, provincial variants). Gap creates urgency for sovereign DC operators who can market compliance today while regulation catches up.11

Key Regulatory Developments
Hydro-Québec Rate Shock — Datacenter Surcharge
New DC rate: 13¢ CAD/kWh (~9.6¢ USD) for DCs >5MW — approximately 2x the current Rate L (~6-6.5¢ CAD). Crypto/blockchain: 19.5¢ CAD/kWh (~3x). Filed by Hydro-Québec to Régie de l'énergie on Feb 19, 2026, following Jan 28 Orders in Council. 5-year phase-in for existing DCs; 3-year for crypto. Bill 69 (sanctioned Jun 7, 2025) requires Ministerial approval for any customer >5MW.23
BC Regulation 8/2026 — Competitive DC Allocation
Effective Feb 1, 2026. Years 1-2: 300MW for AI DCs + 100MW conventional (per-project cap: 145MW). Years 3-4: 150MW AI + 50MW conventional. Crypto mining permanently banned. Assessment includes data sovereignty criterion (jurisdiction of incorporation, data safeguard protocols). Must demonstrate curtailment capability and community benefit. BCE targeting ~35% of Year 1-2 pool.24
